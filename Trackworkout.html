Track Workout for a Better 100m Time

Warm-up

  • 5-10 minutes of light jogging
  • Dynamic stretches (leg swings, high knees, butt kicks)

Why even is it important to warm up?

Physiological Efficiency and Benefits

The primary physiological goal is to prepare the body for the high force and power demands of sprinting.

Muscle Temperature:

A proper warm-up raises core and muscle temperature. Warmer muscles exhibit moremetabolic activity, faster nerve signal transmission, and improved elasticity, which is essential for powerful, rapid contractions and reducing the risk of strains or pulls.

Enhanced Blood Flow and Oxygen Delivery:

Warming up dilates blood vessels, increasing blood flow to the working muscles. This delivers more oxygen and nutrients, improving metabolic efficiency and allowing muscles to work effectively for longer periods.

Neuromuscular Activation:

Sprinting requires rapid and efficient communication between the brain and muscles. A good warm-up, especially with sport-specific drills, activates the central nervous system, leading to improved coordination, faster reaction times, and the recruitment of more muscle fibers for explosive power. This phenomenon is known as Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP), where previous near-maximal muscle contractions can acutely enhance subsequent muscle performance.

Joint Mobility and Tissue Pliability:

Dynamic movements increase the production of synovial fluid, which lubricates joints, and enhances the flexibility and resilience of tendons and ligaments. This allows for a greater range of motion and helps the body better absorb and generate force, further reducing injury risk.

Key Principles for Efficiency

Specificity:

The warm-up should mimic the movements required for sprinting to activate the relevant neural pathways and muscle groups.

Progression:

The intensity must increase gradually to move the body smoothly from a resting state to high-intensity readiness without inducing early fatigue.

Individualization:

Athletes should experiment to find the optimal duration and specific exercises that work best for their body and the given environmental conditions (e.g., a longer warm-up in cold weather).

Mental Focus:

The warm-up period offers a valuable opportunity for mental preparation, helping sprinters to focus, visualize their race, and build confidence.
